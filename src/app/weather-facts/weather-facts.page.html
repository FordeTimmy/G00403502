Weather Facts
  • The highest temperature ever recorded on Earth was 134 degrees Fahrenheit (56.7 degrees Celsius) in Death Valley, California, USA, on July 10, 1913.
  • Lightning can strike the same place multiple times. For example, the Empire State Building in New York City is struck by lightning about 25 times per year.
  • The average thunderstorm releases the energy equivalent of a 20-kiloton nuclear bomb.
  • The world's largest hailstone, measuring about 8 inches (20 centimeters) in diameter and weighing around 1 pound (0.45 kilograms), fell in Vivian, South Dakota, USA, on July 23, 2010.
  • The driest inhabited place on Earth is the Atacama Desert in Chile, where some areas have not received any rainfall for hundreds of years.
  • The coldest temperature ever recorded was -128.6 degrees Fahrenheit (-89.2 degrees Celsius) in Vostok Station, Antarctica, on July 21, 1983.
  • A bolt of lightning can travel at speeds of up to 130,000 miles per hour (209,214 kilometers per hour).
  • The wettest place on Earth is Mawsynram, a village in Meghalaya, India, which receives an average annual rainfall of about 467 inches (11,871 millimeters).
  • The term "hurricane" is derived from the indigenous Taino people of the Caribbean, who referred to their storm god as "Huracan."
  • In a typical year, the Earth experiences about 2,000 thunderstorms every minute, resulting in approximately 16 million thunderstorms annually.
